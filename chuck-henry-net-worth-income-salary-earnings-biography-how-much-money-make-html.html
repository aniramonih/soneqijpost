Chuck Henry (born January 1, 1946, in Los Angeles, California) is a television personality and newscaster who has worked in the news industry for more than 35 years. Henry may be seen co-anchoring the KNBC-TV newscasts at 5, 6, and 11 p.m. on weekdays. Prior to joining KNBC, he spent 19 years at KABC-TV, where he held positions as reporter, anchor, director, and producer (1971-78, 82-93). Henry received national recognition in October 2003, when he and his cameraman were almost murdered in the field while filming a storey on California forest fires. The incident garnered widespread notice. Despite the fact that the fire department had ordered the press media to evacuate, Henry was unable to do so due to a sudden shift in wind direction. The fire devoured and destroyed their vehicle, and the crew had to be rescued by the Los Angeles Fire Department. His first day on the job at NBC4 was January 1994, only a few days after his 48th birthday, when he took over for John Beard as anchor of the 4 p.m. news and also as a reporter for the station. The 6 p.m. broadcast was Henry’s first job after Jess Marlow left the station in 1997, and he has continued to do so every night since Paul Moyer’s retirement in 200Henry is also well-known as the presenter (and executive producer) of Travel Café, a vacation show in which he travels all over the globe, promoting culinary and travel destinations. Travel Café, a two-time Emmy Award-winning television series created in Los Angeles and shown on the Travel Channel, was the first local television series to be produced in high definition. In 1989, Henry presented a brief resurrection of the iconic game show Now You See It on CBS, which lasted just a few episodes. “Eye on L.A.”, a popular travel magazine show that aired from 1984 to 1991, was hosted by Henry, who also served as producer and director in 198As the host of these shows, he has been to every continent on the planet, for which he has garnered multiple Emmy Awards. From 1978 until 1982, Henry worked as a news anchor-reporter at WMAQ-TV, an NBC-owned television station in Chicago, where he gained national attention. From 1966 to 1971, he worked as a news anchor-reporter for KHVH-TV in Honolulu, with a brief stint in Anchorage, Alaska, as a news anchor-reporter for KENI-TV from 1967 to 196He then moved to Los Angeles, where he worked as a reporter for KNBC-TV.
